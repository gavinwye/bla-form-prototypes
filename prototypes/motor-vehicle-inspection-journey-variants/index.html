Apply for a motor vehicle inspection

Journey variant comparison

Five redesigned journeys for the motor vehicle inspection form. The current production journey has 17 steps and 28 fields. Each variant addresses specific completion-rate problems identified in the audit.

Current journey — issues identified

  • 17 steps, no progress indicator — users don't know how far they are
  • 6 consecutive single-field pages (engine number through to vehicle purpose) causes fatigue
  • No eligibility gate — users can complete 80% before discovering they may not qualify
  • Logbook required for 10 pages but no advance warning given
  • Inspection purpose asked first, but never used to branch or reduce fields

V1 — Chunked Sections with Progress Bar

10 pages · ~12 min

Key innovation: Fields grouped into 5 named sections with a visual progress bar on every question page.

Journey map:

Start → About inspection → Your details → Vehicle identity → ID numbers → Numeric specs → Schedule + declare → Check answers → Confirmation

Completion rate assessment:

Moderate improvement. Progress visibility reduces anxiety but the same fields are still present. Low risk for stakeholders — closest to current journey.

V2 — Triage-First Branching

8–12 pages · ~8–10 min

Key innovation: Vehicle category chosen at step 1 determines which technical questions are shown. Agricultural vehicles skip 5+ irrelevant fields.

Journey map:

Start → Triage → Owner → Vehicle identity → Tech (car/goods/agricultural) → Schedule → Declaration → Check answers → Confirmation

Completion rate assessment:

Good improvement for agricultural and motorcycle users (~30% of applicants). Moderate improvement for private car users. Runner-up recommendation.

V3 — Task-Based Hub

6 pages · ~12 min (non-linear)

Key innovation: Three distinct tasks (About you / About your vehicle / Book inspection) that can be completed in any order. "Review and submit" unlocks only when all 3 are done.

Journey map:

Start → Hub (3 tasks) → Task: About you ↔ Task: Your vehicle ↔ Task: Book inspection → Check answers → Confirmation

Completion rate assessment:

Novel but risky. Non-linear flows work well for experienced digital users, but government service audiences skew older and less digitally confident. The hub model may cause confusion about where to start.

V4 — Minimal Pages (Progressive Disclosure)

4 pages · ~10 min

Key innovation: All 28 fields across just 4 pages, grouped with visual section headers. Fewer page transitions means fewer potential drop-off points.

Journey map:

Start → Page 1: Inspection setup → Page 2: You + vehicle (20 fields) → Page 3: Schedule + declare → Check answers → Confirmation

Completion rate assessment:

Fewer pages reduces navigation drop-off but increases per-page cognitive load. Page 2's 20 fields may overwhelm users without their logbook present. Best for digitally confident users.

V5 — Eligibility Gate + Document Checklist

⭐ RECOMMENDED

Key innovation: Front-loaded eligibility check (3 yes/no questions) routes ineligible users out immediately. Document checklist acts as a commitment device — users who tick all 5 documents are psychologically prepared and far less likely to abandon mid-form.

Journey map:

Start → Eligibility check → Ineligible (exit) OR Doc checklist → Steps 1–7 → Check + Submit

Why this generates the highest completion rate:

Factor V5 mechanism
Pre-qualification Ineligible users exit at step 1 — no wasted effort for them or the authority
Commitment device Ticking 5 document checkboxes creates a psychological contract (Cialdini's commitment principle)
Appropriate length 9 active steps (vs 15 current) — below the threshold where completion drops sharply
Cognitive load Technical fields grouped into 3 pages rather than 6 single-field pages
Clear progress Step X of 9 counter gives accurate sense of remaining effort
